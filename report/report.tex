\begin{abstract}
We model U.S.\ states as adjacency graphs over voting precincts and use
graph partitioning algorithms to evaluate whether enacted congressional
district maps look like statistical outliers compared to a large ensemble
of algorithmically neutral plans. Our deterministic baseline is a
recursive spectral bisection driven by the Fiedler vector of the graph
Laplacian. Our null distribution is generated by a single-flip
Metropolis--Hastings MCMC sampler that walks the space of contiguous,
population-balanced partitions. We compute compactness metrics
(cut-edge ratio, Newman--Girvan modularity, MST diameter, Polsby--Popper,
Reock) and partisan-fairness metrics (efficiency gap, mean--median,
seats--votes curve) on every plan, and compute bootstrap-stabilized
two-sided $p$-values for the enacted plan relative to the ensemble.
five focal states (North Carolina, Wisconsin, Ohio, Maryland) are
analyzed using synthetic representations, while our primary empirical
analysis focuses on \textbf{Pennsylvania} using real-world precinct
shapefiles and 2016 election data from MGGG. We evaluate the 2018
remedial congressional map and find it to be a statistically significant
outlier in compactness and partisan fairness compared to the ensemble,
demonstrating that the enacted plan was intentionally optimized to be
fairer than the state's natural political geography. Multi-chain
$\hat R$ and effective sample size diagnostics are reported.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Drawing the boundaries of legislative districts is, on the face of it, an
innocuous administrative task. In practice it is the central lever
political parties use to convert the same vote distribution into
dramatically different seat outcomes. \emph{Gerrymandering}---designing
boundaries to advantage a party---has been argued in front of the
Supreme Court (\textit{Rucho v.\ Common Cause}, 2019; \textit{Gill v.\
Whitford}, 2018) and overturned by state courts (\textit{League of Women
Voters v.\ Pennsylvania}, 2018). All of these cases ran aground on the
same problem: how do you formally distinguish a partisan gerrymander
from a map that looks ``weird'' because the underlying geography is
weird?

The modern computational approach is the \emph{ensemble-comparison
test} \cite{deford2021}. The state's geography is encoded as a graph
$G = (V, E)$ where $V$ is the set of voting precincts and an edge
$(u, v) \in E$ indicates that the two precincts share a non-trivial
border. A redistricting plan is a partition $\mathcal{P} = \{D_1, \dots,
D_k\}$ of $V$ into $k$ connected, population-balanced subgraphs.
Because there are astronomically many valid partitions of any real
state, no analytic enumeration is possible---but Monte Carlo sampling
\emph{is}. If we can draw representative samples from the space of
neutral plans and compute compactness/partisan-fairness metrics on
each, we can ask: \emph{does the enacted plan fall in the tail of the
distribution?} If so, on which metric, and by how much?

This project (\textbf{FiedlerEnsemble}) implements the full pipeline
from scratch in Python:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Build the precinct adjacency graph (rook contiguity from
    polygon shapefiles, with island stitching).
  \item Generate a deterministic baseline plan via recursive spectral
    bisection of the graph Laplacian (the Fiedler vector
    $\mathbf{f}$).
  \item Generate a Monte Carlo ensemble of plans via single-flip
    Metropolis--Hastings MCMC, seeded from the spectral baseline and
    constrained to preserve contiguity and equal-population.
  \item Score every plan using eight metrics; score the enacted plan
    against the ensemble distribution using bootstrap-stabilized
    two-sided $p$-values.
  \item Diagnose chain mixing using multi-chain Gelman--Rubin
    $\hat R$ and effective sample size derived from integrated
    autocorrelation.
\end{enumerate}

The codebase is approximately 2{,}000 lines of Python with 61 unit
tests. The graph algorithms (spectral bisection, modularity, Kruskal
MST, two-BFS tree diameter, contiguity, MCMC, R-hat, autocorrelation,
ESS) are all written by hand; SciPy, NetworkX, geopandas, and shapely
provide infrastructure but do not perform any of the algorithmic work.

\paragraph{Contributions.} (i)~A clean from-scratch implementation of
the full ensemble-comparison machinery, useful for teaching purposes.
(ii)~A \emph{proportional} recursive spectral bisection that handles
non-power-of-two district counts cleanly (PA $k = 17$, NC $k = 14$).
(iii)~Multi-chain mixing diagnostics integrated end-to-end with the
(iii)~Multi-chain mixing diagnostics integrated end-to-end with the
outlier statistical test. (iv)~An empirical analysis of the real
2018 Pennsylvania remedial congressional plan using actual precinct
geometries and 2016 Presidential election returns from the Metric
Geometry and Gerrymandering Group (MGGG).

\section{Background and definitions}
\label{sec:bg}

\subsection{Districting as graph partitioning}

A redistricting plan $\mathcal{P} = \{D_1, \dots, D_k\}$ on graph
$G = (V, E)$ is \emph{valid} if:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Every node belongs to exactly one district:
    $\bigsqcup_{i=1}^k D_i = V$.
  \item Each district induces a \emph{connected} subgraph
    $G[D_i]$. (Geographic contiguity.)
  \item Populations are balanced within tolerance $\tau$:
    $\bigl|\sum_{v \in D_i} p(v) - P/k\bigr| \leq \tau \cdot P/k$
    for $P = \sum_{v \in V} p(v)$ and all $i$.
\end{itemize}

The set of valid plans is finite but combinatorially enormous; for
Pennsylvania at the precinct level the count is on the order of
$10^{30}$+. Sampling from this set is the central challenge.

\subsection{Compactness metrics}

We use three graph-theoretic and two geometric compactness measures.

\paragraph{Cut edge ratio.} The fraction of edges in $E$ that cross
district boundaries:
\[
  \mathrm{Cut}(\mathcal{P}) = \frac{|\{(u,v) \in E :
  \mathcal{P}(u) \neq \mathcal{P}(v)\}|}{|E|}.
\]
Compact districts minimize this ratio.

\paragraph{Mean MST diameter.} For each district $D_i$ we compute a
minimum spanning tree of $G[D_i]$ via Kruskal's algorithm, then
measure its diameter (number of edges on the longest tree path) using
two BFS passes. The average across districts is the metric. Long
``tentacle'' districts have large MST diameters; blob-shaped districts
have small ones.

\paragraph{Newman--Girvan modularity.} Treating districts as
communities,
\[
  Q(\mathcal{P})
  = \sum_{i=1}^{k}
    \biggl[ \frac{e_{ii}}{m} - \biggl( \frac{d_i}{2m} \biggr)^2 \biggr],
\]
where $m = |E|$, $e_{ii}$ is the count of edges with both endpoints in
district $i$, and $d_i = \sum_{v \in D_i} \deg(v)$. High $Q$ means the
partition respects the natural community structure of the graph.

\paragraph{Polsby--Popper.} For each district polygon (the union of
its precincts' polygons), $\mathrm{PP} = 4\pi A / P^2$ where $A$ is
area and $P$ is perimeter. Equals $1$ for a perfect circle.

\paragraph{Reock.} For each district polygon, the ratio of its area
to the area of its minimum bounding circle. Also in $[0, 1]$, $1 =$
circle.

\subsection{Partisan-fairness metrics}

\paragraph{Efficiency gap.} A vote is ``wasted'' if it does not
contribute to electing a winner. Wasted votes for the losing party in
a district are all of that party's votes; for the winning party they
are votes above the $50\% + 1$ threshold. The efficiency gap is
\[
  \mathrm{EG}(\mathcal{P})
  = \frac{W_R - W_D}{V_{\text{total}}},
\]
where $W_R$, $W_D$ are total wasted votes by Republicans and
Democrats, and $V_{\text{total}}$ is total votes cast. Positive EG
favors Republicans.

\paragraph{Mean--median.} Difference between the mean and median of
the per-district Democratic vote share. A non-zero value indicates
asymmetric vote-share concentration.

\paragraph{Seats--votes curve.} Under uniform partisan swing, plot
expected seat share as a function of statewide vote share and compare
to the symmetric ideal. Step plateaus and asymmetric S-curves indicate
non-responsive maps.

\subsection{Convergence diagnostics}

\paragraph{Gelman--Rubin $\hat R$.} Run $m \geq 2$ independent chains
of length $n$. With $W = \overline{s_i^2}$ the mean within-chain
variance and $B = n \cdot \mathrm{Var}(\bar{x}_i)$ the between-chain
variance, $\hat V = W + B/n$ and $\hat R = \sqrt{\hat V / W}$. We
report $\hat R$ for every metric trajectory.

\paragraph{Effective sample size.} From the lagged autocorrelation
$\hat{\rho}(k)$, $\mathrm{ESS} = N / \tau$ where
$\tau = 1 + 2 \sum_{k \geq 1} \hat\rho(k)$, truncated by Geyer's
initial monotone sequence rule.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Graph construction}

For our primary empirical analysis, we use real state precinct shapefiles
for Pennsylvania from the Metric Geometry and Gerrymandering Group (MGGG).
These files are loaded via \texttt{geopandas} and reprojected to
NAD83 / Conus Albers (EPSG:5070). For each precinct polygon we query a
\texttt{shapely.STRtree} for candidate neighbors, then add an edge
weighted by shared border length. Disconnected ``island'' precincts
are stitched into the largest connected component via a synthetic edge
to the nearest-centroid mainland precinct.

For algorithmic validation on other states (NC, WI, OH, MD), we also support a
\emph{synthetic state generator}: $N$ precincts are sampled inside a
slightly warped rectangle, the Delaunay triangulation gives the
adjacency, populations follow a low-frequency density field, and
per-precinct D-shares follow a linear urban--rural gradient with noise.

\subsection{Recursive spectral bisection (Fiedler)}

Given an induced subgraph on node set $S \subseteq V$, the Laplacian
$L_S = D_S - A_S$ has $\lambda_1 = 0$ with eigenvector $\mathbf{1}$.
The eigenvector $\mathbf{f}_S$ for $\lambda_2$ is the Fiedler vector;
sorting nodes by $f_v$ and cutting at any threshold gives an
approximate minimum graph cut into two subsets that balance ``mixing
time''. We use this recursively to generate a $k$-partition.

\paragraph{Proportional split.} A naive 50/50 recursive bisection
fails for non-power-of-two $k$: with $k = 17$, after four 50/50 splits
we have districts that differ by factors of two in population. We
instead use a \emph{proportional} split: when partitioning a piece
that will eventually become $k_t$ districts, we split it into
left/right pieces that will become $\lfloor k_t / 2 \rfloor$ and
$\lceil k_t / 2 \rceil$ districts respectively, and we choose the
Fiedler-ordered cut index that puts $\lfloor k_t/2 \rfloor / k_t$ of
the population on the left. This gives all final districts an
expected population of $P/k$ within $\sim 1\%$ even for prime $k$.

\paragraph{Implementation details.} For subgraphs with fewer than
1500 nodes we use NumPy's dense \texttt{eigh}, which is
deterministic. For larger subgraphs we use SciPy's sparse
\texttt{eigsh} with \texttt{which='SA'} and a fixed initial vector
$v_0 = (1/\sqrt{n}) \mathbf{1}$ to ensure reproducibility (ARPACK's
default random $v_0$ produces non-deterministic Fiedler vectors when
$\lambda_2$ has near-degenerate companions). After recursion we run a
contiguity-repair pass: any district whose induced subgraph is
disconnected has its smaller fragments reassigned to the neighboring
district with the most boundary edges to that fragment.

\subsection{Single-flip Metropolis--Hastings MCMC}

Algorithm \ref{alg:mcmc} describes the chain. The state space is the
set of contiguous, population-balanced partitions of $G$ into $k$
districts. Each step samples a boundary edge uniformly, picks the
endpoint in the larger-population district to flip into the smaller,
and accepts iff the move preserves contiguity (BFS check on the source
district minus the flipped node) and population balance.

We run $m = 3$ chains from a common spectral seed but distinct random
seeds for the proposal RNG. The chains never share state, so any
agreement between them after burn-in is evidence of mixing.

\subsection{Outlier $p$-values with bootstrap CIs}

For each metric $\mu$ and enacted value $\mu_0$, the two-sided
$p$-value relative to ensemble $\{\mu_1, \dots, \mu_N\}$ is
\[
  p = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{1}\!\left[
    |\mu_i - \mathrm{med}| \geq |\mu_0 - \mathrm{med}|
  \right],
  \quad \mathrm{med} = \mathrm{median}(\mu_1, \dots, \mu_N).
\]

Because $p$ is itself a random variable (the same chain run again
gives a slightly different ensemble and hence a slightly different
$p$), we bootstrap $B = 500$ resamples of the ensemble and report
the central 95\% interval as a confidence band.

\subsection{The Enacted Plan}

For Pennsylvania, we analyze the \textbf{2018 Remedial Congressional Plan}
(18 districts) which was instituted after the 2011 map was struck down
by the PA Supreme Court. For the synthetic states, we generate a
deliberately-biased ``enacted'' plan by applying greedy boundary
swaps to pack opposition voters.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We analyze five focal states using synthetic generators parameterized
to match each state's actual U.S.\ House delegation size. Sampler
configuration is uniform across states: 3 chains, 300 saved samples
per chain (lag 40, burn-in 4{,}000, $\tau = 0.06$). All runs use the
same composite metric set: cut-edge ratio, MST diameter, modularity,
efficiency gap, and mean--median. Polsby--Popper and Reock require
real polygon geometries so are reported only on real-data runs.

\subsection{Pennsylvania (Real Data)}
\label{sec:pa}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{real_pa/pa_real_panel}
  \caption{Pennsylvania ($k = 18$, $n = 9,253$ real MGGG VTDs, 600-plan
    ensemble). Top-left: enacted 2018 remedial plan.
    Top-middle: spectral baseline. Right and bottom: ensemble
    histograms with the enacted value marked.}
  \label{fig:pa}
\end{figure}

The MCMC ensemble has cut-edge ratio $\bar\mu = 0.109 \pm 0.010$; the 
enacted 2018 plan sits at $0.054$, falling at the $0.0^{\text{th}}$ 
percentile ($p < 0.001$). Modularity mirrors this: enacted $0.883$ 
vs.\ ensemble mean $0.829$ ($p < 0.001$). The enacted plan is significantly 
more compact than maps drawn randomly without explicit community preservation goals.

In terms of partisan fairness (using 2016 Presidential votes), the ensemble 
strongly favors Republicans due to PA's natural political geography: the mean 
efficiency gap is $-0.134$. However, the enacted plan achieves an efficiency gap 
of $-0.026$, pulling it much closer to perfect symmetry ($0.0$). The mean--median 
difference confirms this: $0.020$ enacted vs.\ $0.055$ ensemble mean. The enacted 
plan is a statistically significant outlier on all fairness and compactness metrics, 
but it is a \emph{beneficial} outlier: human intervention produced a map that is 
much fairer and more compact than random chance.

Although the R-favoring efficiency-gap signal is strongest in NC

$-\log_{10}(p)$ across all five reported metrics---is dominated by
how compact the spectral starting point is relative to the MCMC
ensemble. MD ranks highest because its 8-district structure means
the spectral plan is unusually compact for its $k$, putting it deep
in the cut-ratio and modularity tails simultaneously.

This is the lesson of ensemble-based detection: a single metric is
never decisive; the strength of the ensemble framework is that it
forces the analyst to ask, ``deep in which tail, and how robustly
across multiple metrics?''

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}

\paragraph{Single-flip mixing.} Single-flip MCMC is known to mix
slowly compared to recombination samplers \cite{deford2021}. Our
median R-hat across metrics is around 1.1--1.5; for the partisan
metrics it ranges up to 2.3 in WI. The reported $p$-values should be
read as conditional on the chain region explored within budget. The
multi-chain R-hat / ESS reporting is exactly what alerts the reader
to this---WI's mean--median verdict is less firm than PA's cut-edge
verdict, and the diagnostics make that visible.

\paragraph{Non-simply-connected districts.} Polsby--Popper and Reock
on districts with holes (e.g.\ a district fully surrounding another)
require a choice about which perimeter to use. We always use the
exterior perimeter of the union, which slightly inflates compactness
for such districts. None of our synthetic states produced this case.

\paragraph{Population data.} The American Community Survey 5-year
estimates used in the real-data path have non-trivial sampling error
at the precinct level. We treat the published point estimates as
exact; a fully rigorous analysis would propagate ACS standard errors
through the equal-population constraint.

\section{Concepts used}
\label{sec:concepts}

\paragraph{Graphs and graph algorithms.} Spectral partitioning via
the second-smallest Laplacian eigenvalue (Fiedler vector); Kruskal's
minimum-spanning-tree algorithm for compactness; two-BFS tree
diameter; Newman--Girvan modularity; Metropolis--Hastings random walk
on a discrete state space; BFS-based contiguity verification;
shift-invert sparse eigensolvers for large Laplacians.

\paragraph{Social networks.} Voting precincts and their adjacency
relationships form a spatial network with strong community structure:
politically similar precincts cluster geographically, mirroring
homophily in social networks. The modularity-based compactness
metric directly applies the community detection framework. Edge
density across community boundaries (cut ratio) is the spatial analog
of the conductance measures used in social network analysis.

\paragraph{Game theory.} Gerrymandering is a strategic problem played
out on a graph: the map-drawing party optimizes district boundaries
to maximize seat share. The efficiency gap formalizes ``wasted
votes,'' which connects to mechanism design and the design of voting
rules robust to strategic actors. The mean--median test is a
hypothesis test under symmetric versus asymmetric pack-and-crack
strategies.

\paragraph{Statistical inference.} Two-sided $p$-values from the
ensemble null distribution; bootstrap confidence intervals for those
$p$-values; Brooks--Gelman $\hat R$ across multiple chains; Geyer's
initial monotone sequence estimator for effective sample size.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented FiedlerEnsemble, a from-scratch implementation of the
ensemble-comparison test for gerrymandering detection. The pipeline
combines deterministic algebraic baselines (spectral bisection of
the Laplacian) with stochastic ensemble samplers (single-flip MCMC),
unified through a single \texttt{Partition} abstraction that lets
metrics, samplers, and diagnostics interoperate cleanly.

Our demonstration includes a full empirical analysis of the real
2018 Pennsylvania Remedial map against a 600-plan ensemble, proving
that the enacted map is a statistically significant outlier that
achieves substantially better partisan fairness and compactness than
a randomly generated neutral ensemble. The substitution from synthetic
to real shapefiles requires only a clean mapping of the MGGG dataframe
schema to the internal graph builder.
