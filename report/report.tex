\begin{abstract}
We model U.S.\ states as adjacency graphs over voting precincts and apply
graph partitioning algorithms to evaluate whether enacted congressional
district maps exhibit signs of gerrymandering. By generating large ensembles
of alternative redistricting plans via spectral bisection and single-flip
Metropolis MCMC, we compare each enacted plan against algorithmically
``neutral'' baselines using compactness and partisan-fairness metrics. Our
analysis treats Pennsylvania as the pilot state and reports per-metric
$p$-values quantifying how extreme the enacted plan is relative to the
ensemble.
\end{abstract}

\section{Limitations}
\paragraph{Mixing.} Single-flip MCMC is known to mix slowly relative to
recombination samplers; our results are conditional on the region of plan
space the chain explored within budget.

\paragraph{Single seed plan.} We seed each chain from spectral bisection;
running multiple chains from independent seeds would give stronger evidence
of mixing.

\paragraph{Population data.} ACS 5-year estimates introduce sampling error
at the precinct level.

\section{Concepts used}
\paragraph{Graph algorithms.} Spectral partitioning via Laplacian
eigenvectors, BFS for contiguity, Kruskal's algorithm for minimum spanning
trees, two-BFS tree diameter, Newman-Girvan modularity.

\paragraph{Social networks.} Precinct adjacency forms a spatial network with
community structure; gerrymandering is the strategic distortion of community
boundaries.

\paragraph{Game theory.} The efficiency gap and mean-median formalize
``wasted votes,'' connecting to the design of voting rules robust to
strategic manipulation.
